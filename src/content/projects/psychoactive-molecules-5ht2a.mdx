LSD, psilocybin, DMT, mescaline, and MDMA all act on the same brain target as risperidone, a widely prescribed antipsychotic: the 5-HT2A serotonin receptor. But sharing a target isn't the same as sharing an effect, and the difference comes down to something very physical: how snugly a given molecule sits inside the receptor's binding pocket, and what, specifically, is holding it there. That's a question you can simulate on a computer, atom by atom, before ever touching a wet lab.

## Carving out a pocket to work with

The starting point was a real, experimentally solved structure of 5-HT2A from the Protein Data Bank, captured mid-bind with a prototypical hallucinogen still lodged inside. Stripping that molecule out leaves an empty pocket, which is what a docking simulation needs: a receptor with room to test new candidates.

There was a practical snag. The docking software used here, SwissDock, caps how large a target protein can be, and the full receptor, at over 18,000 atoms, was too big. The fix relies on how the receptor actually sits in a cell membrane: only the portion exposed outside the cell is ever reachable by a drug floating through the bloodstream, while a separate chunk buried inside the cell relays the resulting signal onward. That interior piece could be trimmed away entirely without touching the binding pocket, and a structural alignment against the original, untrimmed receptor confirmed the trim hadn't distorted the pocket's shape.

## Letting six molecules compete for the same seat

With a properly sized pocket in hand, six psychoactive molecules, mescaline, DMT, LSD, MDMA, psilocin, and psilocybin, were each docked in separately, alongside risperidone as a benchmark. Docking works by scanning the receptor for cavities, dropping a candidate molecule into each one at many random orientations, and estimating the binding energy, ΔG, of every resulting pose. A more negative ΔG means a tighter, more thermodynamically stable fit, in the same way a ball settles more stably into a deep bowl than a shallow one.

Four of the six, LSD, DMT, psilocin, and mescaline, landed in the same pocket risperidone occupies, a good sign the simulation is behaving the way real biology does. Risperidone bound tightest, at roughly -10 kcal/mol, with mescaline as the closest competitor about 1.5 kcal/mol behind. In binding-energy terms that's a meaningful gap: each kcal/mol corresponds to a several-fold shift in how favored one pose is over another at body temperature, so 1.5 kcal/mol is the difference between "binds well" and "binds, but noticeably less happily."

Two molecules missed the pocket entirely. Psilocybin's failure has a tidy explanation: it's a phosphorylated molecule that the body has to chemically strip down to psilocin before it can act on this receptor at all, so its inability to dock here isn't a bug, it's the expected negative control. MDMA's failure is less clean. It's known to act on 5-HT2A and shares real structural features with mescaline, so watching it dock instead to an exposed patch outside the pocket is either a genuine hint that MDMA binds somewhere unconventional, or a limit of what this particular docking algorithm can resolve. The paper is honest about not knowing which.

## Testing which contacts are actually load-bearing

Docking tells you where a molecule sits. It doesn't tell you why it stays there. To find out, the next step was to edit the receptor itself, swapping out three pocket-lining amino acids one at a time and re-running the docking simulation with mescaline, the strongest natural binder, each time.

Swapping out an aspartic acid thought to form a salt bridge with mescaline's amine group collapsed the binding almost entirely, matching what's already known about how this receptor grips its usual hallucinogenic partners: remove the charge, remove the anchor. A second swap, targeting a tryptophan expected to contribute a hydrophobic contact, did essentially nothing to the binding energy, directly contradicting prior published claims about that residue. That's not a failed experiment; it's useful, because it means the earlier claim deserves a second look. The third swap went the other direction, trading a hydrophobic residue for a polar one to manufacture a new hydrogen-bond opportunity, and it worked: binding tightened by just over 1 kcal/mol, closing most of the gap to risperidone.

## Why a simulation like this earns its keep

None of this replaces an actual binding assay, and the method has a known failure mode: it occasionally proposes a pose that's geometrically impossible, which is exactly what happened with psilocybin and MDMA. But as a cheap first pass, before committing real lab time and money, this kind of in silico screen is a fast way to ask which residues in a receptor are actually doing the work, and which mutations might make a molecule bind tighter or looser on purpose. With renewed clinical interest in psychedelics for conditions like PTSD, being able to map, quickly and computationally, exactly which contacts a candidate molecule depends on is a reasonable way to prioritize where the expensive, slower experiments should look next. <EndMark />
