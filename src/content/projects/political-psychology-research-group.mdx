Between being an outdoor center leader and an ethical deliberation fellow, I talked to a lot of freshmen, and they always asked the same thing: how do you actually "make it" at Stanford? My answer was always mentorship. The difference between people who felt like they grew and people who just went through the motions and came out with a piece of paper was mentorship, every time. I believe that because of Jon.

He taught me how to find open science. He taught me that obsession and relentlessness have a place in academia right next to kindness. And he taught me the weirder lesson, that if you stare at the world one particular way for long enough, you start seeing the strings it's pulled by in the background. For me that was survey work: why the polls were so bad in 2016, why half of what you read in the *Times* isn't quite what it looks like, and how much of what we call "truth," GDP, inflation, how many people are incarcerated, is really just someone's survey answer.

## The claim we set out to test

For most of the 20th century, good survey research meant probability sampling: pick respondents at random, with known odds of selection, and the math of inference follows. The internet broke that model economically. Random-digit-dial response rates collapsed, and a wave of online "nonprobability" panels, people who opt in rather than get selected, offered faster, cheaper data instead. The catch, replicated across dozens of studies, is that opt-in samples are usually less accurate.

One firm claimed to have found a way around that tradeoff. YouGov's method, sample matching, builds a synthetic sample by drawing a random target sample from a real government survey, then finding opt-in panelists who most closely resemble each person in that target sample on a wide set of characteristics. The pitch is that if the matching variables are rich enough, the matched sample behaves statistically like the random one it's standing in for. YouGov built its Cooperative Election Study, one of the most widely used political surveys in academic research, on exactly this method, and a well-known 2016 Pew study found YouGov beating not just other opt-in panels but some probability samples outright. That result did a lot of work in political science. It's a large part of the reason a matched-sample survey became citable as if it were a random one.

## What we're testing, and why it matters

Jon, my coauthor Gabriel Li, and I set out to check whether that reputation holds up at scale, using nine waves of the CES, 2006 through 2022, checked against real external benchmarks: Census demographic data, verified turnout records, and certified election returns. The design choice that matters most is separating variables the CES explicitly builds its statistical weights around from everything else. The first group will, almost by construction, look accurate. The second is the honest test of whether matching alone is doing what it claims.

The paper is currently under review, so I'm keeping the specifics light here until that process finishes. What I can say is that the CES isn't a niche product. It underpins a large share of published political science research on voting behavior, and its accuracy is frequently assumed rather than checked, in part because of results like that 2016 Pew study. Whether sample matching actually closes the gap between opt-in and random samples, or whether that reputation is doing more work than the method itself, has real implications for how much weight researchers should put on findings drawn from it. <EndMark />
