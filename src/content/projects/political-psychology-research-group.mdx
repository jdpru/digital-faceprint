[Jon Krosnick](https://profiles.stanford.edu/jon-krosnick) was my main undergrad mentor. Between being an outdoor center leader and an ethical deliberation fellow, I talked to a lot of freshmen, and they always asked the same thing: how do you actually "make it" at Stanford? My answer was always mentorship. The difference between people who felt like they grew and people who just went through the motions and came out with a piece of paper — it was mentorship, every time. I believe that because of Jon.

He taught me how to find open science. He taught me that obsession and relentlessness have a place in academia right next to kindness. And he taught me the weirder lesson — that if you stare at the world one particular way for long enough, you start seeing the strings it's pulled by in the background. For me that was survey work: why the polls were so bad in 2016, why half of what you read in the *Times* isn't quite what it looks like, and how much of what we call "truth" — GDP, inflation, how many people are incarcerated — is really just someone's survey answer.

The big project with him was a paper on the CES. Quick version: there's a bunch of ways to sample a survey, random and non-random. We've known forever that asking 100 random people beats asking 10,000 who opted themselves in. But the internet, plus better nonprobability methods, has made non-random samples more and more popular anyway. One company claims to have cracked this with a slick new matching approach. So I spent a chunk of undergrad checking if that's actually true. Mostly, it's not — average error on election estimates comes in above 7%, and it hasn't gotten better in 17 years of data. <EndMark />
