Self-control is the rare idea that everyone believes in and no two people define the same way. To a clinician it is the thing a patient runs out of at the end of a hard day; to an economist it is a discount rate. This project began with a stubborn question: if we can see that pattern in the brain, can we use it — not to describe self-control after the fact, but to predict, before treatment starts, whether a particular person is built to respond to it.

## A study in two acts

The design has two stages, and the second only makes sense because of the first. In the scanner, participants work through a battery of inhibition tasks while we record the neural effort each one takes. Then everyone goes home, and the second act begins: a four-week mobile intervention, measured against the ordinary friction of daily life.

> The wager: if the neural signature captured in the scanner predicts who benefits from the intervention, then "cognitive control" is not just a description of behavior — it is a measurable trait with clinical purchase.

<Display>The scanner tells you what the brain <em>can</em> do. The next four weeks tell you what it <em>will</em>.</Display>

## Four streams, one person

To hold those two worlds together, the study folds four different data streams into a single model of each participant — each one a different resolution on the same underlying question.

| fMRI | Task behavior | Wearables | Daily diaries |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Task-evoked activity in control regions | Stop-signal times and error rates | Sleep and activity | Momentary check-ins |

## What counts as control?

Here the title earns its weight. An *ontology* is a commitment about what exists — about which categories are real. The honest answer is that we don't yet know, and the study is designed to be wrong in informative ways: if a single latent factor does the predictive work, that is evidence for a unified construct; if it takes several, the tidy word "self-control" was hiding a committee. <EndMark />
