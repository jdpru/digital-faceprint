A climate group in Oakland working on urban heat can spend months building something a group across the bay already figured out, simply because there was no way to know the other work existed. The Bay Area climate community, taken together, is sitting on years of pilots, program designs, reports, and hard-won lessons about what actually works. The problem is that almost none of it is findable. It lives in slide decks on someone's laptop, in institutional memory, in PDFs nobody outside the org has ever opened. Euterria, which I co-founded in 2025 with early backing from [Stanford's TomKat Center for Sustainable Energy](https://tomkat.stanford.edu/innovation-transfer/euterria), is the tool we built to fix that.

## A shared, searchable map of what the community already knows

Euterria is a nonprofit knowledge-sharing platform for the Bay Area climate community: a shared resource, in the most literal sense. Organizations upload what they already have, decks, reports, PDFs, and Euterria's pipeline turns those documents into a searchable map of the community's programs, people, and resources. Then anyone can query it the way they'd ask a well-connected colleague, "who's working on wildfire smoke and indoor air quality?", and get a synthesized answer with the actual organizations and initiatives linked underneath. It grows every time a group adds its own material, and it's already become a genuinely useful map of who is doing what across the region.

## The problem underneath the problem

My reference point for this is academia, which has plenty of its own problems, but does one thing very well: it leaves a paper trail. Publishing makes the state of a field legible, and funding, explicitly or implicitly, is tied to how a project builds on and advances what's already been done. In the private sector, a market does similar work: a duplicate product loses to the incumbent, and the incumbent loses to whoever ships something better. Neither of those correcting forces exists for nonprofits. There's no market forcing convergence and no funding structure that specifically rewards knowing what already exists.

Collaboration is often a genuine value for the people running these organizations, well-aligned with their personalities and their mission, but it carries real, mostly invisible costs. Sharing information, digging up an old report, traveling to meet someone, deciding how to coordinate resources, working out who takes on what. It's hard enough inside a single org. Across a dozen of them, it rarely happens at all, not for lack of goodwill, but because nobody has the time to pay those costs.

Long term, I think this needs a funding-level fix, and that's a bigger problem than any one tool will solve. To be clear, nonprofits are service providers, and it's often necessary to have a mesh of organizations serving similar needs across different populations and regions. That's not the inefficiency. The inefficiency is redundant *knowledge*: relearning the same lessons, rebuilding the same playbook, missing the same partner a neighboring org already found. If we can consolidate that knowledge overhead, while still leaving each org free to run its programs its own way, we make real progress toward enriching the nonprofit ecosystem as a whole.

That's a very hard problem, and it will never be solved in its entirety. Euterria started with one piece of it: making discovery itself as smooth as possible. Deciding how to collaborate should be about as easy as searching Google. Knowing who to call should be just as easy.

## Keyword and meaning, blended and reranked

A slide deck or a PDF report isn't structured data, it's a human artifact, full of implicit context and no schema. The actual engineering problem isn't storage, it's turning that mess into something you can ask a real question of, and getting back an answer that's actually useful.

Every document a group uploads gets read by a model and extracted into a typed record: title, issue areas, geography, key people, the program it belongs to, rather than a blob of text nobody can search. That structure is what makes an answer explainable instead of just plausible.

Search itself runs two passes at once: a keyword pass that catches exact matches, and a semantic pass that matches on meaning, including a passage buried deep inside a sixty-page toolkit nobody would have thought to open. Both sets of candidates blend together, and a reranking model makes the final call on order, so the most relevant result surfaces regardless of whether it happened to use the same words as the question.

That last step matters more than it sounds like it should. Two organizations can describe the same work in completely different language, one calls it "urban heat mitigation," another calls it "tree equity." A keyword search alone treats those as unrelated. Reading for meaning is what lets Euterria recognize they're the same conversation.

## In pilot with about twenty organizations around the Bay

Right now, that's about twenty organizations around the Bay, uploading their own material, testing whether the search actually holds up against the way people really ask these questions, and telling us where it still gets things wrong. That feedback loop matters more at this stage than growth for its own sake. We'd rather get discovery right for a tight-knit group of orgs than get it half-right for a large one.

## The platform is powerful, but it's the first step

A search box can surface who's working on what, but it can't make two organizations actually pick up the phone, and it can't build the trust that makes someone share a report before it's officially published. That's the part that has to be built alongside it. The goal isn't for Euterria to become the one place everyone goes to find the person who knows who to call, it's to make that kind of knowledge so easy to surface that every organization can be that person for its own network. Used well, the platform builds a habit: check what already exists before you start rebuilding it. Do that enough, in enough places, and collaboration stops being a special effort and starts being the default way the ecosystem works. <EndMark />
