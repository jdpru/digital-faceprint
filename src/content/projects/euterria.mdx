A climate group in Oakland working on urban heat can spend months building something a group across the bay already figured out, simply because there was no way to know the other work existed. The Bay Area climate community, taken together, is sitting on years of pilots, program designs, reports, and hard-won lessons about what actually works. The problem is that almost none of it is findable. It lives in slide decks on someone's laptop, in institutional memory, in PDFs nobody outside the org has ever opened. Euterria, which I co-founded in 2025, is the tool we built to fix that.

## What it is

Euterria is a nonprofit knowledge-sharing platform for the Bay Area climate community: a shared resource, in the most literal sense. Organizations upload what they already have, decks, reports, PDFs, and Euterria's pipeline turns those documents into a searchable map of the community's programs, people, and resources. Then anyone can query it the way they'd ask a well-connected colleague, "who's working on wildfire smoke and indoor air quality?", and get a synthesized answer with the actual organizations and initiatives linked underneath. So far the platform indexes 117 programs across 14 organizations and 269 people, and it grows every time a group adds its own material.

## The problem it's actually solving

Duplication is expensive everywhere, but it's especially costly in a nonprofit ecosystem where budgets are thin and the work is urgent. Two organizations unknowingly rebuilding the same heat-resilience toolkit isn't just wasteful; it's a collaboration that never happened, a lesson that never got passed on. The usual fixes, conferences, mailing lists, the occasional shared drive, don't scale and don't stick. What's missing is discovery: a fast, low-effort way to find out who else is working on your problem and what they've already learned. That's the entire bet behind Euterria. Make the community's knowledge legible, and collaboration becomes the path of least resistance instead of a lucky accident.

## How it works

The hard part is turning messy institutional documents into something queryable without losing track of where each fact came from. A slide deck isn't structured data; it's a human artifact full of implicit context. Euterria ingests those documents, parses them into structured programs, people, and resources, and embeds them so the whole corpus can be searched semantically rather than by keyword. Queries run as conversational retrieval: the system pulls the relevant material, synthesizes an answer, and cites the specific organizations and initiatives it drew from, so a user can always follow the thread back to the source and reach out. Provenance is the point. An answer you can't trace isn't much use to someone deciding who to call next.

## Backed by Stanford's TomKat Center

Euterria is part of the [TomKat Center for Sustainable Energy's Innovation Transfer portfolio](https://tomkat.stanford.edu/innovation-transfer/euterria), which funds and incubates ventures working toward an equitable, sustainable energy future. That early backing is what let us build Euterria as a free resource for the organizations we serve, instead of another tool with a price tag small nonprofits can't justify.

## Why I care about it

Most of my work has circled the same question from different angles: how do you measure, and make usable, the things that resist measurement? Public opinion, self-control, the marginal ton of carbon. A field's collective knowledge is another one of those things, real and valuable and almost impossible to see all at once. Euterria is the most direct attempt I've made at it, and the most immediately useful. It's a good feeling to build something where "did this help anyone" has a concrete answer. <EndMark />
